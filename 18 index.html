Topic: Angle of Elevation — Two Heights, One Distance

Curriculum: NCERT Ch.9, CBSE, ICSE Class 10

Concepts:

  • tan θ = opposite/adjacent for right triangles
  • Two angles from one point → two equations
  • Special angles: 30°, 60° and their exact values
  • Subtracting heights to find flagpole length

Common Errors:

  • Adding the two angles (30° + 60° = 90°) instead of using tan separately
  • Using sin or cos instead of tan
  • Forgetting that flagpole height = total − post, not total itself
  • Not simplifying 9/√3 to 3√3

Step 1 · The Scene

Imagine a tall vertical post standing on flat ground. On top of it, a flagpole rises even higher. You stand 9 metres away and look up.

To see the bottom of the flagpole (where it sits on the post), you tilt your head up 30°.

To see the top of the flagpole, you must tilt even more — 60°.

Two angles. One distance. Two unknown heights. This is a classic double-elevation problem.

Why two angles from one point? ▼
The observer doesn't move. They look at two different heights on the same vertical line. Each height creates its own right triangle with the same base (9m) but different angles. This gives us two equations with two unknowns.

Step 2 · The Question

We need to find:

  • h = height of the post (from ground to bottom of flagpole)
  • f = height of the flagpole (from post top to flag top)

We know: distance d = 9m, angle to post top α = 30°, angle to flag top β = 60°

The total height (post + flagpole) = h + f.

Step 3 · The Key Insight

In each right triangle, tan θ = opposite/adjacent = height/distance.

For the post alone (angle α = 30°):

tan 30° = h9 → h = 9 × tan 30° = 9 × 1√3 = 9√3 = 3√3

For the total height (angle β = 60°):

tan 60° = h + f9 → h + f = 9 × tan 60° = 9 × √3 = 9√3
Why tan and not sin or cos? ▼
We know the horizontal ground distance (9m) and need the vertical heights. Tan is the only trig function that relates opposite (vertical) to adjacent (horizontal). Sin needs the hypotenuse (line of sight), which we don't know.

Step 4 · Finding the Post Height

From tan 30° = h/9:

h = 9 × 1√3 = 9√33 = 3√3 ≈ 5.20 m

Rationalizing the denominator: multiply top and bottom by √3.

Fill in the blanks:

Step 5 · Finding Total Height

From tan 60° = (h + f)/9:

h + f = 9 × √3 = 9√3 ≈ 15.59 m

Now subtract to get the flagpole height:

f = (h + f) − h = 9√3 − 3√3 = 6√3 ≈ 10.39 m
Why subtract? Why not just use another formula? ▼
We have one equation for total height and one for post height. The flagpole is the difference. We don't need a third formula — subtraction is cleaner and shows the relationship between the two triangles.

Step 6 · The Answers

Post height h = 3√3 ≈ 5.20 m
Flagpole height f = 6√3 ≈ 10.39 m

Verification:

  • tan 30° = 5.20/9 = 0.577 ✓
  • tan 60° = 15.59/9 = 1.732 ✓
  • Flagpole is exactly twice the post height (6√3 vs 3√3)

Step 7 · The Bigger Picture

Final Answers:

  • Height of the post = 3√3 ≈ 5.20 m
  • Height of the flagpole = 6√3 ≈ 10.39 m

This same math is used by:

  • Surveyors measuring tree height + branch height separately
  • Architects calculating building + antenna/spire heights
  • Astronomers measuring mountain peak + snow cap heights

Did you know? The ratio of flagpole to post is exactly 2:1 because tan 60° = 3 × tan 30°. This elegant relationship appears only with these special angles.

Master — Self Test

Variant 1: From 12m away, angle to bottom of flagpole is 30°, to top is 45°. Find post and flagpole heights.

Variant 2: A post is 4√3 m tall. From 12m away, the angle to its top is 30°. A flagpole on top makes the total angle 60°. How tall is the flagpole?

Variant 3: A building has a water tank on its roof. From 20m away, angle to roof is 45°, to tank top is 60°. Find both heights.

Did you know? The Great Trigonometric Survey of India (1800–1900) used exactly this method — measuring angles to the tops and bottoms of distant peaks from known distances — to first determine that Mount Everest was the world's highest mountain.
