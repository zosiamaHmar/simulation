🏆 Challenge · Class 10 · Heights & Distances

The angle of elevation at the top of a building from the foot of a tower is 30°. The angle of elevation of the top of the tower from the foot of the building is 60°. If the tower is 50 m high, find the height of the building.

Difficulty: ●●●○○ · NCERT Ch. 9 · Applications of Trigonometry

Teacher & Parent Notes

Topic: Heights & Distances (Angle of Elevation)

1 The Scene

Imagine standing at the foot of a tall tower, looking up at its top. Now imagine standing at the foot of a nearby building, looking up at the tower's top. Two observers, two angles, one shared distance between them.

The key insight: both the tower and the building stand on the same flat ground. The distance between their bases is the same for both triangles.

2 The Question

We know:

• Tower height = 50 m
• Angle from building foot to tower top = 60°
• Angle from tower foot to building top = 30°

We need to find: Building height = ?

Let the distance between the tower and building be d meters, and the building height be h meters.

3 First Triangle — The Tower

From the building's foot, looking at the tower's top:

tan 60° = oppositeadjacent = 50d

Since tan 60° = √3 ≈ 1.732:

√3 = 50d
d = 50√3 ≈ 28.87 m
This gives us the distance between the two structures. Notice we haven't found the building height yet — we needed this shared distance first.

4 Second Triangle — The Building

From the tower's foot, looking at the building's top:

tan 30° = hd

Since tan 30° = 1√3 ≈ 0.577:

h = d × tan 30° = 50√3 × 1√3
The √3 in the denominator cancels with the √3 from tan 30°!

5 The Calculation

h = 50√3 × √3 = 503
h = 16.6 m ≈ 16.67 m
⚠ Watch out: Many students stop at d = 50/√3 and forget to use it in the second triangle. Always ask: "What else can I find with this value?"

6 The Answer

16.67 m
Height of the Building
Verification: The building is exactly one-third the height of the tower. This makes sense because tan 30° = 1/√3 and tan 60° = √3, so the ratio is 1/3.

7 The Bigger Picture

This same mathematics is used by:

• Surveyors measuring inaccessible heights with a theodolite
• Pilots calculating approach angles to runways
• Architects ensuring buildings don't block sunlight for neighbors
• Astronomers measuring star heights above the horizon

The "two triangles, one shared side" pattern appears in countless problems. Master this, and you've unlocked a powerful problem-solving tool.
