Step 1 · The Scene

Imagine standing at point P on flat ground. You look up at a 10-metre building. The top of the building is 30 degrees above your horizontal line of sight.

Above the building, a flagstaff rises even higher. The very top of that flagstaff is 45 degrees above your horizontal.

Two right triangles share the same base — the distance from you to the building. But they have different heights.

The Bigger Picture

This same mathematics powers:

  • Construction Surveying: Cranes use angle-of-elevation measurements to position loads at exact heights
  • Forestry: Clinometers measure tree heights by sighting to the top and base from a known distance
  • Architecture: Verifying that spires, antennas, and flagpoles meet planned heights after installation
  • Astronomy: Ancient Greeks used this method to estimate the Moon's distance — from two observation points on Earth
Did you know? The Greek mathematician Thales (c. 600 BC) is said to have measured the height of the Great Pyramid by waiting until his own shadow equalled his height, then measuring the pyramid's shadow. He used the same principle: similar triangles with equal angles.

NCERT / CBSE Alignment

Chapter 9 · Some Applications of Trigonometry · Exercise 9.1 · Q12

Learning Objectives

  • Understand angle of elevation as measured above the horizontal
  • Set up and solve two right-triangle equations sharing a common adjacent side
  • Use tan theta = opposite/adjacent with two different opposite sides
  • Calculate a difference of heights to find an unknown segment (flagstaff length)
  • Work with exact values and given approximations (root 3 = 1.732)

Common Errors

  • Adding the two angles (30 + 45 = 75) instead of using them in separate triangles
  • Using the flagstaff angle (45 deg) with the building height (10m) directly
  • Forgetting that tan 45 = 1, making calculations unnecessarily complex
  • Not recognising that both triangles share the same distance d

Prerequisites

  • Trigonometric ratios: sin, cos, tan
  • Special angles: 30, 45, 60 and their exact values
  • Solving linear equations with one variable
  • Working with surds and decimal approximations
